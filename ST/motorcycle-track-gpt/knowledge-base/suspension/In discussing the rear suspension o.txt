In motorcycle racing, the difference between a progressive and a digressive rear suspension shock comes down to how the shock's damping force responds to the speed of the shock's compression and extension.

A progressive rear suspension shock increases the damping force progressively as the speed of the shock’s movement increases. This means the harder or faster the shock is compressed or extended, the more resistance it provides. This type of suspension is beneficial in situations where you expect varied road conditions or obstacles, as it can provide a softer feel during small bumps while stiffening up for bigger hits or during aggressive riding, helping to maintain control and traction.

On the other hand, a digressive rear suspension shock provides a high initial damping force at the beginning of the shock’s movement, which decreases as the speed of the shock’s movement increases. This allows for a firm ride during small bumps or minor road imperfections, offering stability and control at high speeds. However, when encountering larger bumps or obstacles, the damping force decreases, which can lead to a smoother ride over big hits compared to a progressive shock that would stiffen up.

In racing scenarios, the choice between progressive and digressive rear suspension setups depends on the track conditions, rider preference, and the motorcycle's handling characteristics. A progressive setup might be chosen for tracks with a wide range of bumps and obstacles, while a digressive setup could be preferred on smoother tracks where high-speed stability is a priority.

The differences in damping behavior between progressive and digressive rear suspension shocks in motorcycles are achieved through various design and tuning methods within the shock absorber. These methods focus on how the shock manages the flow of hydraulic fluid through its internal valving system in response to movement. Here’s a simplified explanation:

Valving Adjustments: At the heart of both progressive and digressive damping are the valves that control the flow of hydraulic fluid inside the shock absorber. By adjusting the size, shape, and flexibility of these valves, manufacturers can design shocks that either increase resistance with speed (progressive) or decrease it (digressive). Progressive shocks often use valves that become more restrictive with increased fluid speed, while digressive shocks use a setup that allows for easier fluid flow as the speed increases.

Shim Stacks: Shims are thin discs that flex to allow fluid to pass through the shock absorber’s valves. The arrangement and stiffness of these shims can be tuned to create progressive or digressive damping characteristics. For progressive damping, shims are arranged so that more fluid pressure (or speed) is needed to open them, increasing resistance. In digressive damping setups, the shims allow for easy fluid passage at low speeds, with the resistance decreasing as the pressure increases.

Piston Design: The piston moves through the shock cylinder and forces hydraulic fluid through the valves. The design of the piston, including its ports and channels, can influence whether the shock behaves in a progressive or digressive manner. A more complex piston design might allow for a rapid initial flow of fluid that becomes more restricted as speed increases, or vice versa.

Preload and Spring Rate Adjustments: While primarily affecting the spring rather than the damping directly, adjustments in spring preload and the use of springs with variable rates can complement the damping characteristics. For instance, a progressively wound spring (one whose coils are spaced differently along its length) can enhance the progressive feel of the damping by increasing the overall resistance as the suspension is compressed.

Manufacturers often combine these methods to achieve the desired damping profile, and many high-end motorcycle shocks offer adjustable settings.
